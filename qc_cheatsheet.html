Phase Kickback
Setup

Ancilla qubit in \(\lvert - \rangle = (\lvert 0\rangle-\lvert 1\rangle)/\sqrt2\). Oracle \(Q_f\) acts as:

\[ Q_f\lvert x\rangle\lvert y\rangle = \lvert x\rangle\lvert y\oplus f(x)\rangle \] \[ Q_f\lvert x\rangle\lvert -\rangle = (-1)^{f(x)}\lvert x\rangle\lvert -\rangle \]

Phase "kicks back" onto control register. Ancilla unchanged.

Two-case derivation
Case \(f(x)=0\): \[ Q_f\lvert x\rangle\lvert -\rangle = \lvert x\rangle\lvert -\rangle = (-1)^0\lvert x\rangle\lvert -\rangle \] Case \(f(x)=1\): \[ Q_f\lvert x\rangle\lvert -\rangle = -\lvert x\rangle\lvert -\rangle = (-1)^1\lvert x\rangle\lvert -\rangle \]
Oracle unitarity

Q_f is self-inverse: Q_f² = I.

\[ Q_f\lvert x,y\rangle=\lvert x, y\oplus f(x)\rangle \] \[ Q_f(Q_f\lvert x,y\rangle) = \lvert x, y\oplus f(x)\oplus f(x)\rangle = \lvert x,y\rangle \] \[ \Rightarrow\quad Q_f^2=I \]
Oracle types (distinct!)
\[ U_f\lvert x\rangle\lvert y\rangle = \lvert x\rangle\lvert y\oplus f(x)\rangle \] \[ O_f\lvert x\rangle = (-1)^{f(x)}\lvert x\rangle \]

Phase kickback converts standard oracle + |−⟩ ancilla into phase oracle behaviour.

Why it works

\(\lvert - \rangle\) is an eigenstate of \(X\) with eigenvalue \(-1\). Flipping the ancilla when \(f(x)=1\) multiplies the state by \(-1\).

\[ \lvert -\rangle=\frac{\lvert 0\rangle-\lvert 1\rangle}{\sqrt2} \qquad X\lvert -\rangle=-\lvert -\rangle \]
General eigenstate form
\[ U\lvert u\rangle=e^{i\phi}\lvert u\rangle \] \[ \frac{\lvert 0\rangle+\lvert 1\rangle}{\sqrt2}\otimes \lvert u\rangle \xrightarrow{\mathrm{ctrl}\text{-}U} \frac{\lvert 0\rangle+e^{i\phi}\lvert 1\rangle}{\sqrt2}\otimes \lvert u\rangle \]

Boolean case: \(U=X\), \(\lvert u\rangle=\lvert -\rangle\), \(e^{i\phi}=-1\).

Preparing \(\lvert - \rangle\)
\[ \lvert 0\rangle \xrightarrow{X} \lvert 1\rangle \xrightarrow{H} \lvert -\rangle \]
Uses

Deutsch, DJ, BV, Grover oracle, conditional phase shifts (2|0⟩⟨0|−I)

Hadamard-Walsh Transform
Single qubit
\[ H=\frac{1}{\sqrt2} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1\\ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ H\lvert 0\rangle=\lvert +\rangle,\quad H\lvert 1\rangle=\lvert -\rangle,\quad H^2=I \]
n-qubit HWT
\[ H^{\otimes n}\lvert x\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_y (-1)^{x\cdot y}\lvert y\rangle \] \[ H^{\otimes n}\lvert 0^n\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_x \lvert x\rangle \]
Key Properties
\[ \left(H^{\otimes n}\right)^2=I \] \[ \varepsilon_s(x)=(-1)^{s\cdot x} \qquad \text{over }(\mathbb Z_2)^n \]

The n-qubit HWT is the Fourier transform over \((\mathbb Z_2)^n\).

Worked example: \(H^{\otimes 2}\lvert 10\rangle\)
\[ H^{\otimes 2}\lvert 10\rangle = H\lvert 1\rangle\otimes H\lvert 0\rangle = \lvert -\rangle\otimes \lvert +\rangle \] \[ = \frac{1}{2}\left(\lvert 00\rangle+\lvert 01\rangle-\lvert 10\rangle-\lvert 11\rangle\right) \]

Shortcut: \(\mathrm{amp}(y)=(-1)^{x\cdot y}/\sqrt{2^n}\) with dot product mod \(2\).

Complexity

O(n) = O(log N) gates — one H per qubit in parallel.

DFT vs QFT

DFT = classical matrix on a vector. QFT = same transform on quantum amplitudes. Same formula, different object.

You cannot read all QFT output coefficients — measuring collapses to one sample.

Character examples

Simon's Problem over Zⁿ₂
Problem

Given \(f:\{0,1\}^n\to\{0,1\}^n\) with \(f(x)=f(y)\iff x=y\oplus s\). Find \(s\).

Hidden subgroup \(H=\{0^n,s\}\le (\mathbb Z_2)^n\). If \(s=0^n\), the function is injective; otherwise each output has exactly two preimages \(x\) and \(x\oplus s\).

Classical randomized: Ω(2^(n/2)) queries (birthday paradox).

Quantum Circuit
|0ⁿ⟩ ─Hⁿ──────Hⁿ─ measure → yᵢ Of |0ⁿ⟩ ─────────── (ancilla)

After circuit:

\[ p(y)=2^{-(n-1)} \quad \text{if } y\cdot s=0 \pmod 2 \] \[ p(y)=0 \quad \text{if } y\cdot s=1 \pmod 2 \]
State walkthrough
\[ \lvert 0^n\rangle\lvert 0^n\rangle \xrightarrow{H^{\otimes n}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_x \lvert x\rangle\lvert 0^n\rangle \xrightarrow{O_f} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n}}\sum_x \lvert x\rangle\lvert f(x)\rangle \] \[ \xrightarrow{\text{measure 2nd}} \frac{1}{\sqrt2}\left(\lvert x_0\rangle+\lvert x_0\oplus s\rangle\right) \] \[ \xrightarrow{H^{\otimes n}} \mathrm{amp}(y)\propto (-1)^{x_0\cdot y}\left(1+(-1)^{s\cdot y}\right) \]

x₀ unknown/random — doesn't matter. Only the interference pattern encodes s.

Repeat \(n-1\) times to get linearly independent \(y_i\). Gaussian elimination gives candidates \(\{0^n,s'\}\); compare \(f(0^n)\) and \(f(s')\).

\[ P(\text{\(n-1\) LI strings}) > \frac12, \qquad P(\mathrm{fail\ after\ }k\mathrm{\ runs}) < e^{-k/2} \]

Simon's Problem over Z_N
Problem

Promise: \(\exists\) hidden \(L\mid N\) such that \(f(x)=f(y)\iff x\equiv y\pmod L\). Find \(L\). (\(N=2^n\))

Equivalent: f is constant on cosets {x, x+L, x+2L, ...} and different on different cosets.

Why assume L|N?
\[ H=\{0,L,2L,\ldots\},\qquad |H|=\frac{N}{L},\qquad \#\text{cosets}=L \] \[ N=16,\ L=4:\quad \{0,4,8,12\},\ \{1,5,9,13\},\ \{2,6,10,14\},\ \{3,7,11,15\} \]

This makes the Fourier spikes exact: surviving outcomes are multiples of N/L.

Quantum Circuit
|0ⁿ⟩ ─DFT_N─────DFT_N─ measure Of |0ⁿ⟩ ─────────────── (ancilla)
1st DFT_N: make uniform Σ_x|x⟩ Oracle: attach f(x), so equal outputs group x's into a period-L pattern 2nd DFT_N: convert "repeat every L" into frequency spikes Measure: only y compatible with period L survive

After measurement:

\[ p(y)=\frac{1}{L}\quad \text{if } y\equiv 0 \pmod{N/L} \] \[ p(y)=0\quad \text{otherwise} \] \[ \Rightarrow\quad y=i\frac{N}{L} \]

Examples: N=16,L=4 → {0,4,8,12}. N=32,L=8 → {0,4,8,12,16,20,24,28}.

Why only these y survive
\[ \sum_{t=0}^{N/L-1} e^{2\pi iytL/N} = \begin{cases} N/L,& y\equiv 0 \pmod{N/L}\\ 0,& \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]
Postprocessing

Two runs give \(iN/L\) and \(jN/L\).

\[ \hat L=\frac{N}{\gcd(iN/L,jN/L)} \] \[ \text{correct iff } \gcd(i,j)=1 \]

More runs help because gcd strips away the random multipliers i,j,... If all samples share an extra factor, L̂ is too small.

Success probability:

\[ P(\gcd(i,j)>1)\le \sum_p \frac{1}{p^2}\le \frac{11}{12} \quad\Rightarrow\quad P(\gcd(i,j)=1)\ge \frac{1}{12} \] \[ k\text{ runs}:\quad P(\gcd(i_1,\ldots,i_k)>1)=O(2^{-k}), \qquad P(\mathrm{success})\ge 1-O(2^{-k}) \]

Grover's Algorithm
Problem

Search for M marked items among N. f:[N]→{0,1}, f(x)=1 iff x is marked.

Classical: \(\Omega(N/M)\) queries. Grover: \(O(\sqrt{N/M})\).

Circuit
|0ⁿ⟩ ─Hⁿ─[G]─[G]─···─[G]─ measure |0⟩ ─X──H─────────────── |−⟩ (ancilla)

Grover operator:

\[ G = H^{\otimes n}\left(2\lvert 0^n\rangle\langle 0^n\rvert-I\right)H^{\otimes n}O_f \] \[ G = \left(2\lvert \psi\rangle\langle \psi\rvert-I\right)O_f, \qquad \lvert\psi\rangle=H^{\otimes n}\lvert 0^n\rangle \]
Algorithm Steps

1. Init |0ⁿ⟩|0⟩, apply H⊗ⁿ⊗HX → |ψ⟩|−⟩

2. Apply G exactly k = ⌊(π/4)√(N/M)⌋ times

3. Measure n work qubits → solution w.h.p.

Amplitude amplification
Oracle flips sign of marked states Diffusion reflects about |ψ⟩ Together: rotate amplitude from |α⟩ toward |β⟩ Too many rounds overshoot the target

Quadratic speedup only: O(N/M) → O(√(N/M)), not exponential.

Define subspaces
\[ A=\{x:f(x)=0\},\qquad B=\{x:f(x)=1\} \] \[ \lvert \alpha\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt{N-M}}\sum_{x\in A}\lvert x\rangle, \qquad \lvert \beta\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt M}\sum_{x\in B}\lvert x\rangle \] \[ \lvert \psi\rangle = \sqrt{\frac{N-M}{N}}\lvert \alpha\rangle +\sqrt{\frac{M}{N}}\lvert \beta\rangle \]

\(\langle \alpha\vert\beta\rangle=0\), so \(G\) acts in the 2D plane spanned by \(\lvert \alpha\rangle,\lvert \beta\rangle\).

Shor's Algorithm (Factoring)
Problem

Mental model

Same hidden-period blueprint as Simon over Z_N, but not literally the same problem.

Reduction: Factoring → Period Finding

Pick random a with gcd(a,N)=1. Define:

\[ f(x)=a^x \bmod N \qquad\text{with period}\qquad a^r\equiv 1 \pmod N \] \[ r=\operatorname{ord}_N(a) \]

If r is even and aʳ/² ≢ −1 (mod N):

\[ a^r-1\equiv 0 \pmod N \] \[ \left(a^{r/2}-1\right)\left(a^{r/2}+1\right)\equiv 0 \pmod N \] \[ \gcd(a^{r/2}\pm 1,N) \text{ gives a non-trivial factor} \]

Works with prob. ≥ 1/2 over random a. Repeat if r odd or aʳ/²≡−1.

Modular Arithmetic — The Trick
Working with congruences

To verify \(a\equiv a' \pmod M\): write \(a=kM+a'\), then reduce mod \(M\).

\[ a\equiv a' \pmod M,\quad b\equiv b' \pmod M \] \[ a=kM+a',\qquad b=k'M+b' \] \[ ab=M(kk'M+kb'+k'a')+a'b' \Rightarrow ab\equiv a'b' \pmod M \]
EEA Use Cases
Use Case 1: Solve ax + by = c

When \(c=k\gcd(a,b)\). Run EEA to get \(ax_0+by_0=\gcd(a,b)\), then scale by \(k\).

\[ x=kx_0+\frac{b}{d}t,\qquad y=ky_0-\frac{a}{d}t, \qquad d=\gcd(a,b) \]
Use Case 2: Find multiplicative inverse

Find \(x\) such that \(ax\equiv 1\pmod b\). Requires \(\gcd(a,b)=1\).

\[ ax+by=1 \Rightarrow ax\equiv 1 \pmod b \Rightarrow x\bmod b = a^{-1}\pmod b \]

This is how Z*ₘ inverses are computed in practice.

Z*ₘ Inverse — Proof via Bézout

For any \(a\in Z_M^*\), \(\gcd(a,M)=1\). Apply Bézout:

\[ \exists x,y\in\mathbb Z:\quad ax+My=1 \] \[ ax\equiv 1 \pmod M \Rightarrow x\bmod M = a^{-1}\pmod M \]

Replacing \(x\) by \(x-kM\) preserves the Bézout identity.

Example: Z*₁₅
Z*₁₅ = {1,2,4,7,8,11,13,14} 2·8 = 16 ≡ 1 (mod 15) → 2⁻¹ = 8 7·13 = 91 ≡ 1 (mod 15) → 7⁻¹ = 13
Euler's Theorem — Proof Sketch
Setup

Let Z*ₘ = {x₁, x₂, …, xₖ} where k = φ(M). Since gcd(a,M)=1, a ∈ Z*ₘ.

Key idea: multiplication by a is a permutation
\[ a\cdot Z_M^*=\{ax_1,ax_2,\ldots,ax_k\} \] \[ ax_i\equiv ax_j \pmod M \iff x_i=x_j \] \[ \Rightarrow\quad a\cdot Z_M^* \text{ is just a permutation of } Z_M^* \]
Finish
\[ \prod_i x_i \equiv \prod_i (ax_i) \equiv a^k\prod_i x_i \pmod M \] \[ \Rightarrow\quad 1\equiv a^k \equiv a^{\varphi(M)} \pmod M \]
Order Divides φ(M) — Proof Sketch
Goal:

Show r = ord_M(a) divides φ(M).

Division algorithm trick
\[ a^{\varphi(M)}\equiv 1 \pmod M \] \[ \varphi(M)=qr+s,\qquad 0\le s<r \] \[ a^{\varphi(M)}=a^{qr+s}=(a^r)^q a^s \equiv a^s \equiv 1 \pmod M \] \[ \text{Minimality of }r \Rightarrow s=0 \Rightarrow r\mid \varphi(M) \]

Corollary: if p prime, ord_p(a) | p−1 since φ(p)=p−1.

Implication for Shor: the period r of aˣ mod N divides φ(N)=(p−1)(q−1).

Z*ₚ is Cyclic — Proof Sketch
Goal

Show ∃ a ∈ Z*ₚ with ord_p(a) = p−1 (a generator).

Step A — Sum identity
For any M ≥ 1: Σ_{d | M} φ(d) = M Proof: consider fractions 1/M, 2/M, …, M/M. Each reduces to k/d with gcd(k,d)=1. # fractions with denom d = φ(d). Summing over all d|M gives M. ✓
Step B — Nₚ(d) ∈ {0, φ(d)}

Nₚ(d) = #{a ∈ Z*ₚ : ord_p(a) = d}

If Nₚ(d) > 0: ∃ a with ord_p(a)=d. Then {1,a,a²,…,aᵈ⁻¹} are d distinct roots of xᵈ−1 ≡ 0 (mod p). A degree-d poly over a prime field has at most d roots → these are ALL roots. Any b with bᵈ≡1 must be some aᵏ. ord(aᵏ) = d/gcd(k,d) = d iff gcd(k,d)=1 # such k in [0,d−1] = φ(d). → Nₚ(d) = φ(d). ✓
Step C — Conclusion
From B: Nₚ(d) ≤ φ(d) for all d | p−1 Claim: Σ_{d | p−1} Nₚ(d) = p−1 (each element has exactly one order, and every order divides p−1) From A: Σ_{d | p−1} φ(d) = p−1 So both sides equal p−1, and no term exceeds → Nₚ(d) = φ(d) for ALL d. For d = p−1: Nₚ(p−1) = φ(p−1) ≥ 1 → ∃ generator of Z*ₚ ✓
φ(pq) = (p−1)(q−1) — Proof

Count integers in {1,…,pq−1} NOT coprime to pq. Since p,q prime, non-coprime = multiples of p or q.

\[ \#\{\text{multiples of }p\}=q,\qquad \#\{\text{multiples of }q\}=p \] \[ \#\{\text{not coprime to }pq\}=p+q-1 \] \[ \varphi(pq)=pq-(p+q-1)=(p-1)(q-1) \]

Uncomputation
Problem

Reversible computation creates garbage ancillas that entangle with your answer, corrupting interference.

Pattern
1. Compute: |x⟩|0⟩ → |x⟩|f(x)⟩|garbage⟩ 2. Copy useful result to fresh register 3. Run circuit backward (uncompute): → |x⟩|f(x)⟩|0⟩ (garbage erased)

Oracle is self-inverse (O_f²=I), so running it again uncomputes. Used throughout Grover, Simon, Shor.

Clean oracle formula
\[ Q_F^* := Q_F^{-1} \circ \mathrm{CNOT} \circ Q_F \] \[ Q_F^* \lvert x \rangle \lvert 0^a \rangle \lvert b \rangle = \lvert x \rangle \lvert 0^a \rangle \lvert b \oplus F(x) \rangle \] \[ Q_F^* \lvert x \rangle \lvert 0^a \rangle \lvert - \rangle = (-1)^{F(x)} \lvert x \rangle \lvert 0^a \rangle \lvert - \rangle \]

Meaning: compute F(x), copy it out, then erase the workspace. The ancillas return to 0, so later interference is clean.

Exam Traps
❌ Amplitudes = probabilities
Wrong: \(P(x)=\alpha(x)\) Right: \(P(x)=|\alpha(x)|^2\) Amplitudes are complex; probabilities are real ≥ 0.
❌ Superposition = "both answers"

Superposition alone gives no speedup. The power is interference — bad outcomes cancel, good ones reinforce.

❌ QFT reads out all Fourier coefficients

No. QFT encodes them into amplitudes. Measurement samples one outcome. Full readout would destroy the speedup.

❌ Simon / DJ output the secret directly

No. They output constraints (linear equations) on the secret. Gaussian elimination / gcd finishes the job classically.

❌ Hadamard and QFT are unrelated

Both are Fourier transforms: \(H^{\otimes n}\) over \((\mathbb{Z}_2)^n\) with characters \((-1)^{s\cdot x}\), and QFT over \(\mathbb{Z}_N\) with characters \(e^{2\pi isx/N}\).

❌ H²=I means every state is fixed by one H
\(H^2=I\) means applying \(H\) twice returns to start. A state fixed by one H is a +1 eigenstate of H. E.g. \((\lvert 00\rangle+\lvert 11\rangle)/\sqrt2 \to H^{\otimes2} \to\) same state. Orders in Additive Cyclic Groups
In \( \mathbb{Z}_N \) (additive)
\[ \operatorname{ord}(u)=\frac{N}{\gcd(u,N)} \] \[ \operatorname{ord}(u)=\frac{\operatorname{lcm}(u,N)}{u}\quad (u\neq 0) \]

This is additive order, not multiplicative order in \(Z_N^*\).

Direct product rule
\[ \operatorname{ord}_{\mathbb{Z}_{N_1}\times \mathbb{Z}_{N_2}}(u_1,u_2) = \operatorname{lcm}\!\bigl(\operatorname{ord}(u_1),\operatorname{ord}(u_2)\bigr) \]
2-part trick

If \(N=2^Km\) with \(m\) odd, then odd \(u\) keeps the full \(2^K\) in \(\operatorname{ord}(u)\); even \(u\) loses at least one factor of 2.

Order Finding and Factoring
If \(B=PQ\) is already factored
\[ \lambda(B)=\operatorname{lcm}(P-1,Q-1),\qquad \operatorname{ord}_B(A)\mid \lambda(B) \]
Recover the order

Start with \(r=\lambda(B)\). Factor \(r\), then strip a prime \(p\) whenever \(A^{r/p}\equiv 1 \pmod B\). The final \(r\) is the exact order.

Complexity link

Factoring \(\Rightarrow\) order-finding efficiently. Shor gives order-finding \(\Rightarrow\) factoring. So the two problems are tightly linked.

Shor lucky case

If the relevant component orders have different powers of \(2\), then the period is even and the final gcd step is likely to produce a nontrivial factor.

Cosets and HSP
Coset facts
\[ |xH|=|H|,\qquad xH=yH \text{ or } xH\cap yH=\varnothing \]

Cosets partition the group.

Hidden subgroup promise

\(f\) is constant on cosets of \(H\) and distinct on different cosets.

\[ f(x)=f(y)\iff x^{-1}y\in H \]
Course examples

Simon over \((\mathbb{Z}_2)^n\): hidden subgroup gives linear constraints. Simon over \(\mathbb{Z}_N\) / Shor: hidden period gives Fourier spikes.

Graph Isomorphism via HSP
Automorphism subgroup

\(\operatorname{Aut}(G)\le S_n\). Permuting a graph and reading off its adjacency matrix gives a function constant on right cosets of \(\operatorname{Aut}(G)\).

Disjoint union trick

For \(G=G_1\sqcup G_2\), the graphs are isomorphic iff some automorphism of \(G\) swaps the two halves.

Consequence

An efficient HSP algorithm on \(S_{2n}\) would give generators for \(\operatorname{Aut}(G)\), and that would solve Graph Isomorphism efficiently.
